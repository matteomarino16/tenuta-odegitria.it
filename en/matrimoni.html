Tenuta Odegitria: a wedding in a real trulli village

You were looking for authentic Puglia. Not the brochure version, not a photo set built to look like it. You were looking for living territory — trulli, vineyards, the Valle d’Itria — and you wanted to get married inside it, not in front of it.

When you arrive at Tenuta Odegitria, something happens that’s hard to explain before you live it: the road leaves the main highway, the landscape opens onto hills and vineyards that seem still in the Puglian afternoon, and in front of you appears a unique 18th-century trulli village.

It’s not a venue that acquired trulli as a scenic element. These are authentic 18th-century trulli, with dry-stone walls, white cones, and courtyards that tell centuries of life in this corner of Puglia the world knows as Valle d’Itria.

There are things you notice right away when visiting wedding venues in Puglia.
And there are things you only understand later, when you sit down and think about what you saw.

What you notice right away:

The trulli, the vineyards, copper lights, tables set with white flowers.

It’s beautiful. It’s recognizable.

It’s — let’s be honest — already seen.

What you realize later:

  • That most venues have a single main area, with extra spaces added over time to handle increasing demand.
  • That the ceremony happens in one place, then everyone gets in the car to go sign at the town hall, then back, then waiting, then finally it starts.
  • That the catering is the venue’s, chosen from a catalogue — not in reality.
  • That if it rains, Plan B is a white tent in the parking lot.
  • We’re not talking about bad venues. We’re talking about compromises that become visible only when it’s too late to change them.

Venue exclusivity

What does it mean to have the venue exclusively?

Not only that there are no other weddings at the same time or right after, but also that there are no other minor events or outside guests in the property on your wedding day — for example, people staying in rooms sold to occasional customers.

You’ll certainly want to avoid curious onlookers and gatecrashers on your big day, just as your guests would never want to be forced to rush their coffee because they’re asked to leave to make room for the evening shift.
